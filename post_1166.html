When I was studying German, I worked on reading Goethe's Faust in an
edition that had .de and .en on opposing pages. One appeal of doing
something like that is that the .de is 'better' because the .en
translator has an 'impossible' task in trying to be both accurate and
'literary'. That is, the reader may think, "I wouldn't say it like
that." pretty often.

The other positive of such reading is that if one has a robust .de/.en
dictionary, one finds themselves reading the dictionary a lot, which is
what the .de/.en v. is supposed to spare you from. A paradox.
